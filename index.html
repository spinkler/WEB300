Welcome to a Convergent Media Experience

brought to you by Three Ominous Cats

This Web Media Project examines the convergence of media over time, with a particular (but not sole) focus on gaming technologies. It is designed in an asynchronous manner, allowing for a non-linear experience, however a clear narrative will present itself if you choose a linear viewing experience instead.

We also encourage our audience to start discussion on any points of interest using the comment section at the bottom of each page. Do you remember what it was like back then? How is convergence affecting your life now? Are you prepared for when convergence seeps into every facet of human life?

Production Diary

Ty Scicluna

Weekly

Meetings were held on Discord twice per week, assuring our schedule was adhered to. This allowed each team member to offer feedback and advice on othersâ€™ work, ensuring that each member was part of the complete production while maintaining separate roles. A specific schedule audit was conducted during Week 7 to highlight potential issues, but deadlines were met without issue. The fundamental framework of our design allowed individuals to work completely independently, and the whole team worked very well to deliver a complete, punctual project.

Week 5

Implementation of this project began with establishing a git account to contain our source code. This ensured logging changes to the code base while providing a means for the team to access the code. Once set up the directory structure would be created and pushed to git to offer a starting point, and we proceeded to choose our design.

Week 6

Implementation of navigation buttons and aesthetics began, offering a fundamental structure to work with when implementing page animations and transitions.

With previous HTML and CSS experience, Javascript was completely missing from my skill set. Javascript was necessary to provide a clean, smooth user experience, or our site would lack narrative and direction, so research began.

Material design guidelines were researched, particularly for animation. These animations offer a cohesive, natural, and intuitive experience, making the flow of information obvious.

Week 7

After installing an IDE (Integrated Development Environment) to keep track of code implementation of Javascript began, but had issues. Initially an animation framework was used. This proved tricky, and was ultimately unsuitable for our needs. It was time to get my hands dirty and start building this from the ground up.

Learning Jquery was my goal, so I began by implementing a basic framework in HTML and CSS. I have experience in many programming languages and with CSS animations so I had some idea of what to expect. The first Javascript I implemented resolved the page we were coming from, allowing me to determine which page state to transition from, and hence, the beginning state of the next animation.

Week 8

After implementing transitions I encountered a glaring problem. When tested remotely the slower speed prevented smooth transitions. Rendering subsequent pages was incredibly obvious and broke the user experience, neglecting our fundamental goals.

Back to the drawing board, I learned more about Javascript/Jquery, and how to implement a proper SPA (Single Page Application). This required a major overhaul of the code base. Luckily all of the referrer logic established earlier would carry over. Dissatisfied with the colour scheme, this reimplementation also consisted of assigning all the CSS colour definitions to variables so they could be freely altered.

Week 9

With our SPA fully implemented and showcasing smooth transitions and navigation, week 9 was all about polish. First, colour was given full attention. This was simple thanks to the variables implemented earlier, so we iterated until we found something suitable.

I reviewed the Material Design animation guidelines to polish delays and durations of all the animations to offer the most cohesive experience possible, and image modals were implemented, allowing users to view images in high resolution.

With the heavy lifting done, it was now simply a matter of importing and proof reading the content, registering DNS, uploading, and testing our code.

Nathan Barnes

Week 5

During the first production week, we met via Discord to discuss our proposed WMP as set during the draft phase, and all assigned duties were understood before we began our work. I then conducted preliminary research into the articles we wanted to present on the website that would be our WMP. My role, inclusive of writing articles, editing and formatting with addition of acquiring attributable, quotes and images, I had to ensure all content was easily accessible and transferable from my copy to the website. Therefore, I utilised the Google Suite I had shared with the team during the draft phase to allow team members the opportunity to provide feedback, access content and see individual progress.

Week 6

For this week, I focused on additional article research and compiled the research I had into drafts based upon each of our nine set topics. I sourced information via Google, feeling it proved efficient in locating the information required to help form the article I had selected to write. Although I have experience in, and enjoy the task of creative writing, I had minor difficulties keeping to the word limit we, as a team, set for each article to ensure we met the user experience of 10-minutes or less. I overcame this by writing in my usual style, and applying heavy editing skills, which made for concise articles as planned.

Week 7

This week I was heavily focused on content development for the website. I began editing my draft articles, formatting for easy website transference, implementing breakout quotes and images with attribution and then finalising the article content. This ensured it was a presentable web standard and the standard we as a team were striving for and found acceptable. In addition, being midway into the WMP we as a team took time to assess and ensure we were meeting our set deadlines and assigned duties. We found this to be a constructive task as it identified we were an effective team that worked well together, and were effective communicators capable of achieving our set goal.

Week 8

This week I began by focusing on articles written by other team members and suggesting edits and feedback I felt applicable. During this week, I was also able to offer feedback to the current website development, which afforded a learning opportunity, as I was able to learn a small amount of Javascript, and Jquery that Ty was implementing into our WMP. Given my entry-level understanding of HTML and CSS, it was of interest to me to see the code behind our WMP that made it function, which afforded me a learning experience outside of my defined skill set and knowledge.

Week 9

During the last week of production, final touches to the WMP ensured our content met the requirements of a 10-minute or under viewing experience. I wrote the final article assigned to me, as per our production schedule in addition to raising the question of copyright and creative commons (CC) usage on DB. The matter of copyright and CC I discussed with the team as I felt it of great importance given our use of images and materials not of our own creation, which therefore, I felt required careful consideration. In addition, I compiled a reference list for all used quotes, images and video files used within all articles, adhering to required referencing style.

Matt Simpson

Week 5

The first week of production has been light in actual content creation and instead, much of individual effort was dedicated into going over the brief and aligning my thoughts to where each team member was headed for the next couple of weeks as per suggested by the production schedule. During the first of our weekly meetings, I helped to set up some of the foundations of WMP creation regarding shared documents, templates and discussed who specifically was writing which article. Nathan and I also evaluated design mock-ups that Ty had created and suggested feedback and direction for future work in the layout of the WMP.

Week 6

In this week of production, I attempted to put much of my focus on building a partnership with Ty as my role required me to assist in some of the web development aspects of the WMP. I created a GitHub account to collaborate, and provide feedback to Ty, in practice however, we found it much more productive to allow him to control all coding as modifying or changing existing code could create confusion. Instead, I allocated this saved time into researching ways in which we could implement a â€˜commentsâ€™ feature into the articles, eventually settling on and suggesting the Facebook comments plugin.

Week 7

This week I carried out bulk, general background research into the chosen areas of content production as predetermined by my role. This process required me to read, and gather multiple articles on topics that we have previously agreed upon to write about. With this information freshly in mind, I created the first article in the blog — "An Unlikely Resurrection for the Arcade", and requested Nathan to provide feedback on the work in Google Docs. This article took more time to finalise than originally planned in the production schedule as I was aiming to use the standards of it as a benchmark for preceding work.

Week 8

Immediately upon receiving detailed feedback from Nathan for my article, I started writing and further published two more articles — "Always-Online", and "The Historical Significance of the MMO" using the sources found in the previous week. Alike to my previous content, these were submitted to Google Docs for review by Nathan. I found that my previous research, and previous efforts in writing the first article had set the groundwork to delivering these two articles in quick succession.

Week 9

The final week revealed itself to be slightly different to what I had planned as there were major topic changes to the last two articles, the reason being that the initially planned articles had too much in common to what was already written by myself and Nathan. This meant I had to allocate more time earlier in the week to have these articles properly researched, written and reviewed from the ground up. Finally, I could help the team in adding to the final touches to the presentation which included testing the website, suggesting several references to content and providing general feedback to the design to Ty before he delivered the final upload of our WMP.

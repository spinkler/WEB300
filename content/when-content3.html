When Technology Converges with People

by Matt Simpson

By now we are well familiar with implantable technology as inventions such as the cardiac pacemaker have been around, and saving lives since the 1960s. Recently, sci-fi titles such as Deus Ex: Human Revolution have popularized and painted an awe-inspiring picture of what a world with 'human augmentation' would look like regarding physical enhancements to the body.

The Black Mirror series portrays a much grimmer reality of technological implants in the episode 'Playtest', where the protagonist takes part in an experiment, accepting a neural implant to alter his perceived real-world senses into an augmented reality video game. The barriers between the game and the real-world are completely blurred into one reality, and the protagonist was subjected to a horrific experience crafted from his greatest fears.

As of recently, these fictional scenarios have come closer to being a reality with the collaboration of Arm and Center for Sensorimotor Neural Engineering (CSNE) in presenting early plans for developing a 'brain implantable system-on-a-chip' (SoC). Rather than fabricating and subjecting patients to nightmare-ridden realities, the organisations are aiming to improve the quality of life to those with mental disorders such as Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease. The implants, if successful, should be useful in decoding and altering messages that the brain receives to correct the behaviour by the brain to help those suffering live better lives.

Whilst a future reality of human augmentation may often be portrayed by the media as a misstep for the human race, each day there are behind-the-scenes progressions in medical fields which may, like the cardiac pacemaker, improve the lives of many.

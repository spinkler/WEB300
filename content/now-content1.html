Always Online

by Matt Simpson

As the Internet has become ubiquitous in the lives of many, we may now find ourselves in an 'always-online' state with our smartphones, wi-fi enabled notebooks and the digital games we play. Whilst it is understandable that some games such as Dota 2 with its online centric gameplay may require a constant Internet connection, there are often others such as Diablo 3 and many mobile games that require Internet connectivity with no clear benefit to the player.

Diablo III's "Error 37" would achieve meme status as overloaded servers prevented players from playing the single player campaign upon launch

To step back for a moment, an always-online game can offer many supposed features to benefit the player such as patch rollouts, microtransactions, social connectivity and additional content. However, developers (especially larger ones) have realised an opportunity to embed the 'Always-on DRM' feature into popular games which are primarily there to prevent digital piracy. The earliest versions of Diablo 3 and SimCity (2013) were both notable examples of always-on DRM, facing the wrath of furious gamers who were unhappy with this new direction that developers were taking. Frustrated gamers stated that requiring these popular games to be always-online ruined their experiences with the games for reasons such as the instability of the game's servers during peak periods, and furthermore, the fact that these games cannot be played with the absence of an Internet connection.

Whilst the Internet clearly offers benefits to gamers when it is correctly augmented into digital games, we may need to look closer into including it in a way that doesn't impede the experience of the gamer.

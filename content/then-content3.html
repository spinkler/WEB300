The Historical Significance of the MMO

by Matt Simpson

Ask an academic about their thoughts on the MMO (Massively Multiplayer Online game), and they'll often give reasons beyond nostalgia or addictive gameplay as to why they were, and still are such a big deal. It is after all, that the MMO changed the rules around the activity of 'play' and how it is further defined. Previously academics such as Johan Huizinga (1938) proposed several conditions to be met for 'play' to emerge, and one of these was that participants were required to be physically situated with one another.

This primitive activity of 'play' has been changed only within some of our lifetimes with the earliest renditions of MMOs somewhat resembling MUDs (Multi-User Dungeons), and later growing into the popular MMORPG (Massively Multiplayer Online Role-Playing Game). For first time ever, gamers were enabled to engage in 'play' and communicate with others in other geographical regions.

Notable early titles such as Ultima Online and Everquest placed the player in vivid virtual worlds which were usually fantasy rich and based in medieval periods. This social and deep approach to gaming was well received with MMORPGs as they continue to grow in popularity and evolve alongside technological advances.

Upon skipping back to the present day, an observation may be that most modern video games borrow from these earliest renditions in ways which work, or sometimes don't. One thing is for sure, and that is that MMOs have forever changed the way that gamers engage in 'play'.
